## What They Care Most About (Ranked)

### 1. Morphing Injuries Detection
Steve emphasized this multiple times. Workers comp claims often "morph" - someone presents with a back injury, then 3-4 months later claims their shoulder also hurt. Having the original recorded statement lets them "roll back the tape" and prove what was originally reported.

> "Workers compensation, one of the challenges we always have are injuries that morph... what that allows us to do is kind of roll back the tape."

**Implication:** Transcription + search + comparison features are high value.

### 2. Litigation Defense
~15% of claims become litigated. Plaintiff attorneys ask for golden parachutes, but recorded statements help establish facts. Defense attorneys need the recordings for depositions.

> "The facts of the recorded statement do not support a claim even close to that value."

**Implication:** Easy sharing/export of recordings and transcripts to attorneys is important.

### 3. Surveillance Identification (Video Value)
This was a unique insight. They do surveillance on suspected fraudulent claims (people claiming permanent disability but actually working). **Problem:** Can't do surveillance if you can't identify the person. Many workers (construction, depots) have no photo on file.

> "Surveillance doesn't have value if you cannot identify the worker... being able to identify him and capture video, which can only be done if you know what he looks like, I see on camera meetings as your number two point in your presentation, that brings a lot of value."

**Implication:** Video is not just nice-to-have — it enables fraud detection. This is a powerful sales message.

### 4. Adjuster Training & QA
Steve reviews adjuster files to provide feedback. Transcripts would help him:
- Identify missed questions
- Evaluate statement-taking skills
- Train junior adjusters

> "Taking statements is a skill. And it's one that's difficult to teach."

He gave a great example of new adjusters awkwardly leading up to the alcohol/drug question vs. naturals who just ask it.

**Implication:** QA dashboard, coaching features, training mode are all high value.

### 5. Policyholder Evidence
Unexpected use case: They sometimes need to SEE the injury scene — equipment, stairs, etc. — to determine compensability. Video calls with employers could capture this.

> "Very often there's value in us actually, for example, seeing a piece of equipment, they were injured on the stairs."

## What They Liked

1. **Video capability** — Enables surveillance identification (huge for fraud)
2. **Outlook integration** — Adjusters live in Outlook
3. **Calendar sync preventing double-booking** — "That's huge for the adjuster"
4. **Automatic transcription included in price** — They currently have to pay separately for transcription
5. **Pricing model** — $100/month/adjuster with unlimited recordings resonated well
6. **Claimant self-scheduling** — Multiple time options, claimant picks, times gray out when taken
7. **No app download required** — Just a link, works on mobile

## What They Didn't Like / Concerns

1. **File format uncertainty** — WMV vs MP4 compatibility with their claim system
2. **Video reluctance** — Adjusters may be uncomfortable; some claimants may be reluctant (age factor)
3. **SOC 2 / PII handling** — Need documentation for compliance
4. **No phone-only option** — They asked if all statements have to be video (can turn off camera, but still joins video call)
5. **Reminder limitations** — Calendar-based reminders may not be enough for workers in the field

---

## Their Current Process (Pain Points)

1. **Scheduling:** Adjusters phone out 3 consecutive days to schedule (best claims practices)
2. **Recording:** Phone system records, adjuster types notes in dialog box during call
3. **Post-call:** Recording saved as WMV, manually attached to claim file
4. **Summarization:** Adjuster manually summarizes key facts
5. **Transcription:** Only done when claim is litigated, costs money, goes to separate department

**Pain:** Manual process, no automatic transcription, hard to go back and review, can't identify claimants for surveillance.
